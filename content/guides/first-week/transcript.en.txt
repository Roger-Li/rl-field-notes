New Dad Field Guide — Everything You Need to Know

Induction, Labor, Newborn Care, Postpartum Recovery. Sourced from AAP, CDC, ACOG, HRSA, NHTSA, and peer-reviewed literature.


The Induction

For low-risk first-time pregnancies, elective induction at 39 weeks has evidence support. The ARRIVE trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2018, found that elective induction at 39 weeks in that specific group actually reduced C-section rates compared to waiting. That does not mean every 39 to 40 week induction is automatically recommended, but if this is the plan, you're still in very standard territory.

How induction typically works

The process usually happens in stages. Your OB will pick methods based on how "ready" the cervix is, measured by something called a Bishop Score.

Step one is cervical ripening. A prostaglandin pill called misoprostol is placed vaginally or under the tongue, or a balloon catheter is inserted into the cervix. This usually happens the evening before and runs overnight.

Step two is Pitocin IV. A synthetic oxytocin drip is started, which causes contractions to begin or strengthen. This typically happens the morning after ripening.

Step three is breaking the water, also called AROM. The doctor uses a small hook to rupture the amniotic sac. It's usually quick and may feel like pressure or a gush of fluid, though experiences vary. This happens when the cervix is dilated enough.

There is also a variable step, the epidural. Anesthesia is placed in the spine for pain management. It's the partner's choice and not a failure. It can happen whenever she requests it.

Warning: Expect it to take a while. For first-time mothers, inductions commonly take 24 to 36 or more hours from the start of cervical ripening to delivery. Many hospitals admit you the evening before. Pack entertainment, snacks, and patience.


Hospital Bag

You may be there 2 to 4 days total, counting induction plus recovery. Pack for both of you, plus baby's going-home outfit.

For her, bring ID, insurance card, and hospital paperwork. Bring a birth plan in printed copies. A comfortable robe or nursing-friendly gown. Slippers with grip plus flip flops for the shower. Lip balm, because hospitals are very dry. Hair ties and toiletries. A nursing bra and breast pads. A going-home outfit in maternity size, since she'll still look about six months pregnant. A phone charger with a long cable. And a pillow from home — put a colored case on it so it doesn't get confused with hospital pillows.

For you, bring a change of clothes for 2 to 3 days. Toiletries and deodorant. Snacks like protein bars, nuts, and jerky. A phone charger and a portable battery. Cash for vending machines or the cafeteria. Entertainment such as a book, tablet, or headphones. Comfortable shoes. A jacket, because hospitals run cold. And a pillow plus blanket, since your "bed" will be a chair.

For baby, bring an infant rear-facing car seat. A going-home outfit in both newborn and 0 to 3 month sizes. A receiving blanket. A hat and socks or mittens. Diapers and wipes — the hospital usually provides them but bring backup. And a swaddle blanket.

Tip: Install the car seat now. You'll need an appropriate rear-facing car seat for discharge, and some hospitals may ask you to show safe setup before you leave. Install it before labor, read both the car-seat manual and your vehicle manual, and use an NHTSA-certified inspection station if you want a second set of eyes.


Your Role in Labor

Your job is simple in concept, hard in execution: be present, be calm, be her advocate. You can't take the pain away, but you can make it more bearable. Here's a concrete breakdown.

During early labor, from zero to six centimeters, which can last hours to a day or more, she'll feel mild to moderate contractions and can still talk through them. Your job is to keep her hydrated, fed if allowed, and relaxed. Time contractions. Watch shows together. Let her rest.

During active labor, from six to eight centimeters, which lasts about 2 to 8 hours, contractions become strong and come 3 to 5 minutes apart. She can't talk through them. Apply counter-pressure on her lower back. Offer ice chips. Give breathing cues and verbal encouragement. Don't take mood changes personally.

During transition, from eight to ten centimeters, which lasts about 30 minutes to 2 hours, this is the most intense phase. Nausea, shaking, and saying "I can't do this" are all common. Stay close. Take it one contraction at a time. Hold her hand. Wipe her face. She may yell at you — that's normal and not personal.

During pushing, at ten centimeters, which can last 20 minutes to 3 hours, she'll feel an urge to push and intense pressure. Support her position by holding a leg or supporting her back. Count during pushes if she wants. Encourage her. Stay near her head unless she wants you elsewhere.

Concrete things to say and not say

Things you should say: "You're doing amazing." "One contraction at a time." "I'm right here." "What do you need right now?" "Breathe with me," and then actually breathe slow. "Whatever you decide is the right call," especially regarding the epidural.

Things you should not say: "I know how you feel," because you don't. "Calm down" or "Relax." "My mom says you should..." "Are you sure you need the epidural?" Anything about how tired, hungry, or bored you are. And don't narrate what you see happening down there unless asked.

Note: You're also her translator. Know her birth plan. If she can't speak during contractions, you communicate her preferences to the nurses and doctors. This is one of the most valuable things you can do.


Newborn Care

Newborns are simultaneously tougher and more fragile than you'd expect. Here's the stuff that actually matters in the first week.

Immediately after birth

Skin-to-skin contact happens right away. Baby goes directly on mom's chest. This regulates baby's temperature, heart rate, and breathing. AAP recommends at least 1 hour. Dad gets skin-to-skin too — do it as much as possible.

The first breastfeed is usually attempted within the first hour. Don't panic if it doesn't go perfectly.

APGAR scores are taken by nurses at 1 and 5 minutes, measuring appearance, pulse, grimace, activity, and respiration on a scale of 0 to 10. Seven or above is normal.

Newborn procedures include a vitamin K shot to prevent bleeding disorder, erythromycin eye ointment, and a hepatitis B plan before discharge.

Newborn screening includes blood-spot screening for the conditions on your state's panel, a hearing screen, and a CCHD pulse-ox screen that measures blood oxygen to detect heart defects. All three should happen before discharge.

Weight and measurements are taken. Average is 6 to 9 pounds and 19 to 21 inches. Some weight loss is expected in the first days, but if the loss is getting into the upper single digits or baby keeps losing after day 5, expect a closer feeding check.

Daily care basics

For diaper changes, wipe front to back, especially for girls. Wait for the umbilical cord stump area to dry. Use petroleum jelly on the circumcision site. Expect 8 to 12 changes per day.

For umbilical cord care, keep it dry and clean. Fold the diaper below it. It falls off in 1 to 3 weeks. No rubbing alcohol is needed — that's outdated advice. Check it every diaper change.

For bathing, do sponge baths only until the cord falls off. Use warm water and mild soap. Support the head and neck at all times. Bathe 2 to 3 times per week at most.

For temperature checks, a rectal thermometer is the gold standard for newborns. Normal is 97.7 to 99.5 degrees Fahrenheit, or 36.5 to 37.5 degrees Celsius. Check when baby feels warm or cool.

For holding and supporting, always support the head and neck. The neck muscles are very weak for the first roughly four months. Do this every single time.

For tummy time, start within days of birth. Do short sessions of 3 to 5 minutes while baby is awake and supervised. This builds neck and core strength. Do it several times a day.

Jaundice — what you need to know

Most newborns develop some degree of jaundice, a yellowing of the skin and eyes, as their liver matures. It typically peaks around days 3 to 5. The hospital should measure bilirubin levels before discharge, and your pediatrician will recheck at the first visit.

For adequate output by day 5, look for at least 6 wet diapers and at least 3 stools in 24 hours. Fewer can mean baby is not taking enough in, which worsens jaundice.

For color progression, mild yellowing of the face is common. If it spreads to the chest, belly, or legs, or the whites of the eyes turn yellow, call your pediatrician.

If the baby is sleepy and feeding poorly, a jaundiced baby who is hard to wake or feeding poorly needs same-day evaluation.

For follow-up, make sure you leave the hospital with a clear bilirubin result or recheck appointment, usually within 1 to 2 days of discharge.

Warning: Do not use sunlight to treat jaundice. An old wives' tale says to put baby in sunlight. This is not a substitute for medical treatment and risks sunburn and temperature instability. If phototherapy is needed, your pediatrician will arrange it.

Tip: The 5 S's are your secret weapon for soothing. From Dr. Harvey Karp's method, widely endorsed by pediatricians: Swaddle, which is a snug wrap. Side or Stomach position, for holding only and not for sleeping. Shush, meaning loud white noise. Swing, meaning gentle rhythmic motion. And Suck, using a pacifier or finger. These mimic the womb and can stop crying fast.

Note: Schedule the first pediatrician visit. AAP recommends baby be seen 3 to 5 days after birth and within 48 to 72 hours of hospital discharge. Book this appointment now if you haven't already.

Warning: RSV protection is a before-discharge question. CDC now frames this pretty simply: babies should usually be protected by one of two options — a maternal RSV vaccine during pregnancy, or an infant RSV antibody. Most babies do not need both. If mom did not get the maternal vaccine, her status is unknown, or baby was born within 14 days of maternal vaccination, ask before discharge whether your baby should get a long-acting RSV antibody this season. Babies born during RSV season in most of the U.S. who need it usually get it within 1 week after birth, ideally during the birth hospitalization.

Visitors and infection control

You are the bouncer. Newborns have immature immune systems, so every visitor is a potential exposure.

No visits from anyone who is sick, period. Everyone washes hands before touching baby, bottles, or pump parts. Tdap and flu vaccines should ideally be given 2 or more weeks before meeting baby. Anyone with cold symptoms stays away — RSV, flu, and COVID all count. No smoking anywhere near the baby, and smoke residue on clothes counts. Limit visitor count and duration — mom needs rest, not an audience.


Feeding

Feeding is the number one thing you'll be doing. Whether breast or formula, here's what to know.

Breastfeeding

Interesting fact: breast milk doesn't "come in" for 2 to 5 days. Before that, the breasts produce colostrum — a thick, yellow, nutrient-dense liquid that's basically liquid gold for the baby's immune system. It comes in tiny amounts, but a newborn's stomach is only marble-sized, so it's enough.

On day 1, the baby's stomach is about the size of a cherry, roughly 5 to 7 milliliters. Each feed is about 1 to 2 teaspoons. You should see at least 1 wet diaper and 1 dirty diaper.

On day 2, the stomach grows to about the size of a walnut, roughly 15 milliliters. Each feed is about half an ounce. Expect at least 2 wet and 3 dirty diapers.

On day 3, the stomach is walnut-to-apricot sized, about 25 milliliters. Each feed is about half an ounce to 1 ounce. Expect at least 5 wet and 3 dirty diapers.

On day 4, the stomach is about the size of an egg, roughly 45 to 60 milliliters. Each feed is about 1 to 2 ounces. Expect at least 6 wet and 3 dirty diapers.

On days 5 through 7, the stomach is still about egg-sized. Each feed is 1 to 2 ounces. Expect 6 or more wet diapers and 3 or more dirty diapers, which should be yellow and seedy.

By week 2 and beyond, the stomach is about the size of a peach. Each feed is 2 to 3 ounces. Expect 6 or more wet diapers, and stools will vary more.

Note: How you help with breastfeeding. You can't physically do this part, but you are critical to its success. Bring her water — she'll be incredibly thirsty. Get pillows arranged. Burp the baby after feeds. Track feeds in an app. Handle all diaper changes you can. Learn to spot hunger cues — rooting, which is turning the head, lip smacking, and fist sucking — before baby starts crying, since crying is a late hunger signal.

Formula feeding

For the amount, after the first few days expect about 2 to 3 ounces every 2 to 3 hours, roughly 20 ounces per day.

For the type, always use iron-fortified formula, as recommended by the AAP.

For preparation, follow the instructions exactly. Don't dilute or concentrate. Use room-temperature or warm water and never microwave.

For vitamin D, if breastfed, give 400 IU liquid vitamin D drops daily, starting in the first few days. If formula-fed, once baby drinks 32 or more ounces per day of formula, supplementation isn't needed.

Warning: Powdered formula is not sterile. For babies under 2 months, premature, or immunocompromised, CDC says extra precautions matter. Powdered formula is not sterile. When feasible, ready-to-feed liquid formula is the safer option. If you use powdered formula, mix it with water hot enough to kill potential Cronobacter bacteria, about 158 degrees Fahrenheit or 70 degrees Celsius, then cool before feeding. Always discard any formula left in the bottle after a feed — never save and reheat it.

Burping

Burp after every 2 to 3 ounces of formula or when switching breasts. Three positions work: over your shoulder, sitting on your lap with chin supported, or face-down across your lap. Pat gently but firmly on the back. Some burps take a minute — be patient.


Safe Sleep

This is life-or-death important. In 2022, about 3,700 U.S. babies died suddenly and unexpectedly. Safe-sleep rules matter because sleep environment is one of the biggest preventable risk factors you can control.

The ABCs of Safe Sleep, from the AAP 2022 Guidelines.

A is for Alone. No blankets, pillows, bumpers, stuffed animals, or other people in the sleep space. Nothing but baby and a fitted sheet.

B is for Back. Always on their back. Every nap, every night. Until age 1. Even if they seem to prefer their side or stomach.

C is for Crib. A firm, flat mattress in a safety-approved crib or bassinet. No incline. No Rock 'n Play. No couch. No adult bed.

Room-share but don't bed-share. Baby sleeps in your room but in their own crib or bassinet. This reduces SIDS risk by up to 50 percent. Ideally for 6 months, at minimum.

Move baby after dozing off elsewhere. Car seats, strollers, swings, infant carriers, and slings are not safe for routine sleep. If baby falls asleep there, move them to a firm, flat sleep surface as soon as possible.

No loungers, nests, or nursing pillows for sleep. Skip Boppy-style nursing pillows, DockATot-style loungers, nests, pods, sleep positioners, and inclined sleepers. If it is not a safety-approved sleep space, it is not for sleep.

No weighted swaddles or sleep products. No evidence they help, and they may increase risk.

Use a wearable blanket, also called a sleep sack. It keeps baby warm without loose fabric and is safer than blankets.

Stop swaddling when baby shows rolling signs. This usually happens around 3 to 4 months. A swaddled baby who rolls to their stomach can suffocate.

Offer a pacifier at sleep time. This is associated with reduced SIDS risk. If breastfeeding, wait until breastfeeding is established, around 3 to 4 weeks.

No smoking around baby. Secondhand smoke is a major SIDS risk factor.

Don't let baby overheat. Dress them in one layer more than you'd wear. Feel their chest — if it's sweaty, they're too warm.

Warning: The most dangerous moments. The highest risk for sleep-related death is in the first 6 months, and it often happens when an exhausted parent falls asleep holding the baby on a couch or recliner. If you're too tired to stay awake, put the baby down in the crib. A briefly crying baby in a safe crib is infinitely safer than a sleeping baby on your chest while you doze off on a couch.

Newborn sleep patterns

Newborns sleep 16 to 17 hours a day, but in 1 to 3 hour bursts. They have no concept of day versus night. You cannot train a newborn to sleep through the night — they need to eat too frequently. Your strategy is survival: sleep when the baby sleeps and take shifts with your partner.


Before Discharge

The hospital discharge is where things fall through the cracks. Adrenaline is fading, you're sleep-deprived, and suddenly you're responsible for a tiny human at home. Print this list or screenshot it.

For baby

Newborn screening should be complete. Blood-spot screening, hearing screen, and CCHD pulse-ox screen should all be done. Because blood-spot screening is state-based, ask whether your state requires a repeat screen if the first one was early or if your baby is in a two-screen state.

Have a bilirubin and jaundice plan. Get the bilirubin result or a written plan for when to recheck. Jaundice peaks at days 3 to 5 — you may already be home.

Have the feeding plan confirmed. Whether breast, formula, or combo, get clear guidance on frequency, amount, and who to call if latching isn't working or supply is a concern.

The pediatrician visit should be booked. It should be 2 to 3 days after discharge, or around 3 to 5 days of age. Don't leave without this appointment scheduled.

Have the hepatitis B plan confirmed. If mom is HBsAg-positive or status is unknown, the birth dose is time-sensitive and should be given within 12 hours. If mom is HBsAg-negative, CDC now uses individualized decision-making; AAP still recommends giving the first dose within 24 hours.

Have the RSV protection plan confirmed. Most babies should be protected either by maternal RSV vaccine during pregnancy or by an infant RSV antibody. If mom did not get the maternal vaccine, her status is unknown, or baby was born within 14 days of maternal vaccination, ask whether baby should get the antibody now. If needed during RSV season in most of the U.S., it is usually given within 1 week after birth.

Make sure the car seat is ready for discharge. Rear-facing in the back seat, installed according to the seat and vehicle manuals. Hospital staff may review setup, and an NHTSA inspection station can help if you want it checked.

For mom

Prescriptions should be filled. Pain meds, stool softeners, iron supplements — whatever was prescribed. Fill them before leaving or have someone pick them up so they're ready when you get home.

A follow-up visit should be scheduled. ACOG recommends contact within 3 weeks and a comprehensive visit within 6 to 12 weeks. If she had hypertension or preeclampsia, a blood pressure check should happen within 7 to 10 days.

Warning signs should be reviewed. Make sure you, not just her, know the big ones: heavy bleeding soaking at least 1 pad per hour, clots bigger than an egg, bad-smelling discharge, chest pain, trouble breathing, severe headache, vision changes, one-sided leg swelling, seizure, or thoughts of self-harm or harming the baby.

Have feeding support contacts ready. Get the lactation consultant's number, the hospital's breastfeeding hotline, or the pediatrician's feeding advice line.

Know who to call

Note: Save these numbers in your phone now. Before you walk out, make sure you have: the pediatrician, both daytime and after-hours, the OB or midwife, both daytime and after-hours, a lactation consultant, the hospital nursery (many answer questions 24/7 in the first few days), and Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.

Tip: The 48-hour rule. If anything feels off — baby's feeding, color, breathing, or mom's pain level, bleeding, mood — and you can't clearly say "this is normal," call. In the first week, pediatricians and OBs expect calls. You are not bothering them. That's literally what they're there for.


Caring for Mom

Here's the thing nobody tells dads directly enough: your partner just went through a major medical event. Even a "normal" vaginal delivery involves significant tissue damage, blood loss, and hormonal upheaval. Your job for the first 6 weeks is to be her teammate, protector, and personal assistant.

Physical recovery — what to expect

Vaginal pain or tearing is common. Most women have some degree of tearing. Stitches dissolve on their own in 1 to 2 weeks. Sitting is painful. To help, have ice packs ready. Set up her peri bottle to squirt warm water while peeing. Get a donut pillow. Don't rush her to do anything physical.

Bleeding, called lochia, involves heavy vaginal bleeding for 2 to 4 weeks, tapering off. It's similar to a very heavy period as the uterus sheds its lining. Stock up on heavy-duty maternity pads, not tampons. Don't be alarmed by clots — they can happen, but clots larger than an egg need urgent attention.

Uterine cramping happens as the uterus contracts back to normal size. It's worse during breastfeeding because oxytocin triggers contractions. These are called afterpains. Over-the-counter ibuprofen can help — ask her OB. A heating pad on the belly also helps.

Breast engorgement occurs when milk comes in around day 3 to 5. Breasts become hard, swollen, and painful. Use warm compresses before feeding and cold compresses after. Help her get the baby latched quickly. Have a lactation consultant's number ready.

Sweating and hormonal shifts are common. Night sweats, hair changes, and mood swings all come from the massive progesterone and estrogen drop after delivery. Keep extra sheets nearby. Be patient. This is biochemistry, not personality.

Constipation is very common. Fear of the first postpartum bowel movement is real. Have stool softeners like Colace on hand, plus high-fiber foods and lots of water. Encourage her not to hold it.

If delivery becomes a C-section

About 1 in 3 U.S. deliveries are cesarean. If this happens, recovery is slower and your role shifts significantly.

The hospital stay is 2 to 3 days, longer than vaginal delivery. She'll need help getting in and out of bed, walking to the bathroom, and holding the baby for feeds.

No lifting more than baby's weight for 6 to 8 weeks. You become the default lifter, carrier, and driver. Car seats, strollers, laundry baskets — all you.

For incision care, watch for redness, swelling, warmth, or oozing at the incision site. She should keep it clean and dry.

For pain management, she'll likely have prescription pain meds initially, then transition to ibuprofen or acetaminophen. Track the schedule.

If she had hypertension or preeclampsia

Warning: Blood pressure follow-up is critical. ACOG recommends a blood pressure evaluation no later than 7 to 10 days postpartum for hypertensive disorders of pregnancy. Postpartum preeclampsia can develop up to 6 weeks after delivery — don't skip this appointment even if she feels fine.

Your concrete daily tasks

Cook meals or organize meal deliveries. Handle all visitors and gatekeep her rest. Do laundry — you'll go through so much laundry. Bring her water every time she nurses. Change diapers and take the night shifts you can. Handle groceries and errands. Take baby so she can shower, nap, or exist as a human. Keep the house reasonably clean. Manage communication with family, including updates, photos, and boundaries. Tell her she's doing a great job — and mean it.

Mental health — the big one

Warning: Baby blues versus postpartum depression. Baby blues affect 70 to 80 percent of mothers. Symptoms include mood swings, tearfulness, anxiety, and irritability. It starts a few days after birth and resolves within 2 weeks. This is normal hormonal adjustment.

Postpartum depression affects 10 to 15 percent of mothers. Symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks and include persistent sadness, withdrawal from baby, inability to sleep even when baby sleeps, loss of appetite, feelings of worthlessness, or thoughts of self-harm. This requires professional help immediately.

The AAP recommends screening with the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale at 1, 2, 4, and 6 months. You don't need to wait — if you notice these signs, bring it up gently and contact her OB.

Note: Don't forget about your mental health. Paternal postpartum depression is real and affects roughly 8 to 10 percent of new dads. Sleep deprivation, identity shift, relationship stress, and feeling helpless are all legitimate struggles. If you're not okay, seek help too.

ACOG recommends postpartum evaluation within the first 3 weeks after delivery, either by phone or in person, with a comprehensive visit within 6 to 12 weeks.


Red Flags

Most issues are normal newborn stuff. But some things require immediate medical attention. Knowing these in advance means you won't panic about normal things and you won't miss actual emergencies.

Baby — Call the pediatrician or go to the ER

A rectal temperature of 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit or 38 degrees Celsius or higher: In a baby under 3 months, any fever is an emergency. Don't wait. Don't give Tylenol first. Go to the ER.

Difficulty breathing: Look for nostril flaring, rib retractions where the skin pulls in between ribs, grunting, or bluish lips or skin.

Won't eat or refuses multiple feeds: Newborns should not go more than 4 hours without feeding. Lethargy plus refusal is urgent.

Too few wet diapers for baby's age: Think in 24-hour totals — about 1 on day 1, 2 on day 2, 5 on day 3, then 6 a day by day 4 to 5. Once feeds are established, a sudden drop in urine output is urgent.

Jaundice worsening: Some yellowing is normal. But if it spreads to the belly or legs, or baby is very sleepy and hard to wake, call immediately.

Forceful or projectile vomiting: Spitting up is normal. Vomiting that shoots out is not.

Extreme lethargy: Hard to wake, floppy, unresponsive — this is an emergency.

Cord bleeding that won't stop: Some spotting is okay. Active bleeding or foul smell means infection.

Mother — Call her OB or go to the ER

Heavy bleeding or egg-sized clots: Soaking through 1 or more pads in an hour, passing clots bigger than an egg, or passing tissue is urgent.

Bad-smelling discharge or fever of 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit or 38 degrees Celsius or higher: This can signal uterine infection, wound infection, or mastitis.

Severe headache or vision changes: This could be postpartum preeclampsia, which can happen up to 6 weeks after delivery. It's especially urgent with swelling, high blood pressure, or shortness of breath.

Chest pain or trouble breathing: Pulmonary embolism and other serious causes need emergency care now.

One-sided leg swelling, pain, or redness: This could indicate a blood clot, called deep vein thrombosis. Postpartum women are at higher risk.

Seizure, fainting, or sudden confusion: Call 911. These are medical emergencies.

Thoughts of harming herself or the baby: Get emergency help now. Postpartum psychosis is rare but serious. You can reach the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline by calling or texting 988.

Warning: The fever rule. For both mom and newborn, 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit or 38 degrees Celsius is the magic number. Below that, monitor. At or above that, call a doctor immediately. For babies under 3 months, a fever is always an emergency — they can deteriorate rapidly because their immune systems are immature.

Normal things that look scary

A weird-shaped head is from molding through the birth canal. It rounds out in days.

Baby acne or red blotches are hormone-related and go away on their own.

Black tarry poop called meconium is normal for the first 1 to 2 days. It transitions to green, then yellow.

Startling or jerky movements are the Moro reflex, a totally normal neurological response.

Hiccups are very common and don't require intervention.

Crossed eyes happen because the eye muscles are still developing. This is normal until about 4 months.

Sneezing is just clearing nasal passages, not a cold.

Breast swelling in the baby is from maternal hormones and goes away quickly.
