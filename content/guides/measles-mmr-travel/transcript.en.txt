Measles, MMR, and Travel With Babies — U.S., April 2026

If you are traveling with a baby, the key measles questions are practical, not political: when routine MMR starts, when a 6 to 11 month infant should get an early travel dose, what counts as actually protected, and what to do right away after an exposure.

This guide was checked against CDC measles guidance on April 19, 2026. Case counts can change weekly.


Quick answers before we begin.

First, routine MMR starts at 12 to 15 months.

Second, babies 6 to 11 months old who will travel internationally should get one early MMR dose before departure.

Third, that early dose does not count toward the routine two dose series after the first birthday.

Fourth, after an exposure, call ahead immediately. MMR may help within 72 hours and immune globulin may help within 6 days.


Why now.

Measles is back on many U.S. parents' radars in 2026. On April 17, 2026, CDC reported 1,748 confirmed U.S. cases as of April 16, 2026. That is the dated snapshot behind this guide. Case counts can change week to week, so treat the number as a marker, not a forecast.

Measles is one of the most contagious viruses known to medicine. The virus can linger in the air and on surfaces for up to 2 hours after an infected person leaves the space, and it spreads easily to anyone nearby who is not immune. In a fully susceptible group, a single case can infect roughly 9 out of every 10 close contacts.

The hardest group to protect is babies under 12 months — too young for the routine first MMR dose. For families with infants, the practical questions are narrow: when does routine protection start, when does a baby need an early travel dose, and what do you do right away if you think you've been exposed.


Routine MMR schedule.

The routine MMR schedule in the U.S. is two doses. Dose 1 is at 12 to 15 months. Dose 2 is at 4 to 6 years. That pattern does not change just because you are traveling.

There is one practical travel exception for toddlers. If your child is already 12 months or older and has only had the first MMR dose, dose 2 can be given earlier than the usual 4 to 6 year window for international travel, as long as it has been at least 28 days since dose 1. Ask your pediatrician whether accelerating dose 2 makes sense for your trip.

For a broader look at the full childhood schedule, see our Childhood Immunization Guide for the U.S. in 2026.


The early travel dose for infants 6 to 11 months.

This is the key decision for families with an infant and an international trip on the calendar. The short version: if your baby is 6 to 11 months old and will travel internationally, CDC recommends one MMR dose before departure.

For babies 0 to 5 months, there is no MMR travel dose. Call your pediatrician about exposure planning and destination risk.

For babies 6 to 11 months, one early MMR dose before international travel. The baby still needs two more doses later on the routine schedule.

For children 12 months and older, try to finish age-appropriate two dose protection before travel if possible, and remember that dose 2 can be accelerated 28 or more days after dose 1.

On timing: CDC advises getting the travel dose ideally at least two weeks before departure, so the immune response has time to develop. If the trip is sooner than that, still get a dose. Partial protection is better than none at all.

One important warning. An MMR dose given before the first birthday does not count toward the routine two dose series. A baby who gets an early travel dose at, say, 9 months, still needs the regular dose at 12 to 15 months and a second dose at 4 to 6 years. That is three total shots for that baby, not two.

One more boundary. CDC does not recommend measles vaccine for infants younger than 6 months. For babies in that age range, protection relies on avoiding high risk exposures, making sure every adult in the household is up to date, and calling your pediatrician immediately if exposure is possible.


What "protected" actually means.

"We probably had it" is not a protection plan. Before any trip, know what actually counts as evidence of immunity for every traveler in your party.

Acceptable evidence of immunity includes written vaccine records showing age-appropriate MMR doses, lab evidence of immunity such as a positive measles IgG test, lab-confirmed prior measles infection, or, for U.S. adults, birth before 1957.

Not enough: family memory without written records, vague recollections like "I think I had the shot as a kid," or a childhood rash that was never lab-confirmed.

For most international travelers born during or after 1957, two documented doses of MMR is the standard to aim for. Infants getting an early travel dose at 6 to 11 months are the explicit exception — one dose before travel, then two more on the routine schedule.

A helpful parent note: if an adult traveler is unsure whether they were fully vaccinated and can't find a written record, CDC says there is no harm in getting another MMR dose. Titers are an option too, but for most families another shot is faster, cheaper, and settles the question.


Before international travel — the timeline.

Most of the work happens before you leave the house. Use this timeline in reverse — the earlier you start, the more options you have.

As soon as the trip is booked, check everyone's vaccine records, not memory. You want documents in hand before calling the pediatrician.

Two or more weeks before departure, get needed MMR doses if possible. That gives the immune response time to develop before departure.

Less than two weeks before departure, still get a dose if not protected. Partial protection is better than none.

Before leaving, check the CDC destination page and travel notices. Local outbreaks and itinerary risks change fast.

After return, watch for symptoms for three weeks. Measles can incubate up to about 21 days after exposure.

Pull written records, not memory. Your pediatrician or pharmacy can print a vaccine record. Many states also have an online immunization registry. A screenshot or printout you can show a clinician beats any conversation that starts with "I think…"


After exposure or suspected exposure.

Call your pediatrician or clinic immediately and say you may have had a measles exposure. Do not walk into a waiting room without calling ahead. Walking into a shared waiting room with suspected measles can infect other vulnerable patients. Most clinics will route you to a separate entrance, triage by phone, or send you to the right place.

Post-exposure options are time-sensitive. Two possible preventive measures exist after a known or suspected exposure, and both have windows.

MMR may help if given within 72 hours of exposure, for eligible contacts who are not already protected.

Immune globulin may help if given within 6 days of exposure, and is typically considered for infants, pregnant people without evidence of immunity, and severely immunocompromised patients.

The choice between post-exposure MMR and immune globulin depends on age, immune status, pregnancy, and how long ago the exposure was. Your pediatrician, the clinic, and local public health decide. Your job is to call fast and share the exposure details accurately. MMR and immune globulin should not be given at the same time.

What measles looks like. Symptoms usually appear 7 to 14 days after exposure and follow a distinct pattern. First, for about four days, there is fever, cough, runny nose, and red watery eyes — the three C's. Around days three to five, tiny white spots called Koplik spots can appear inside the cheeks. Then, from days four to seven, a red blotchy rash starts on the face at the hairline and spreads downward to the trunk, arms, and legs.

Same-day action, not next-week action. If measles is possible — fever plus rash, recent international travel, known exposure to a case — treat it as a same-day call. Stay home, keep the baby home, and phone before going anywhere.


Travel checklist — the final list.

A short pass before you lock the suitcase. If anything here is a no, that is the item to resolve first.

Check written vaccine records for every traveler.

If your baby is 6 to 11 months and traveling internationally, ask for an early MMR dose.

If your child is 12 months or older and not fully vaccinated, ask whether dose 2 should be accelerated before travel.

Save your pediatrician's after-hours number.

Watch for fever and rash for three weeks after return.

If measles is possible, stay home and call before going in.
