Induction Diary, Part 1.

Mid-to-Late Pregnancy.

At 24 weeks I passed the glucose tolerance test with flying colors. By 28 weeks I'd gained only about 2 to 3 kilograms from my pre-pregnancy weight. The 30-week growth scan put the baby's estimated weight right at the median — everything was tracking around the 50th percentile except for a larger-than-average biparietal diameter.

Late Pregnancy, Weeks 30 to 36.

Between weeks 30 and 36, my belly grew noticeably fast and everything felt much heavier. I still kept up my routine — gym at least three times a week, 30 minutes each session, and mostly healthy meals — but between the pregnancy sweet tooth and work pressure, I didn't fully commit to a diabetic-style low-carb diet. In the last two months of the third trimester, right after the holiday season, I finished writing an R01 as lead PI, completed major revisions on three papers, submitted a new manuscript, managed two ongoing MPI projects, and dealt with various co-author papers. At the 36-week growth scan, on top of the already-large head, the baby had jumped to the 75th percentile for weight at 3.2 kilograms, and the 89th percentile for abdominal circumference. Devastating.

On my OB's recommendation, I learned about the ARRIVE Trial — elective induction at 39 weeks — and decided to make it our birth plan. We chose to try an early vaginal delivery over a scheduled C-section, since the latter is more invasive even though the outcome is more predictable.

From 36 Weeks.

Stretch marks appeared overnight and multiplied by the day. The strict sugar and carb control also took a toll on my mental state. But the numbers suggested it was working: during the controlled period the baby gained roughly 200 grams per week after 36 weeks, compared to about 250 grams per week during the uncontrolled stretch from weeks 30 to 36.

39-Week Checkup.

The cervical exam showed a soft cervix with the baby's head sitting low — good conditions for induction. My OB said we probably wouldn't need the full series of scary-sounding procedures. Even though I studied medicine in undergrad, learning how to perform procedures on patients versus being the patient yourself are very different experiences. Sublingual misoprostol plus IV Pitocin should be enough. The whole discussion left us feeling pretty pumped.

Waiting for Admission.

Since this was an elective induction with no medical emergency, we were added to the hospital's waitlist with no guaranteed admission time. With the mindset of "already full-term, the sooner the better," we spent the following days on call, checking in with the hospital daily, until we finally got the word: come in at 39 weeks and 4 days, early morning. My partner and I packed up our massive suitcase, nerves and excitement buzzing, and headed to the Labor and Delivery building we'd studied so many times during our prenatal prep.
