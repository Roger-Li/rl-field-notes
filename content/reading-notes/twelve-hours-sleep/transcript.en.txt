A Step-by-Step Plan for Baby Sleep Success

About the Author

Suzy Giordano, known as "The Baby Coach," started sleep training babies in 1992 when a close friend had triplets through IVF. She offered to help, and the method was born. She began with triplets and higher-order multiples (where sleep deprivation is existential-level), then expanded to twins, and eventually singletons. She's a mother of five, including fraternal twins, and has trained over 1,500 babies across the US, UK, Australia, and India. The method has been tested on singletons, twins, triplets, babies with colic, reflux, cleft palates, and developmental challenges — and Giordano claims it has never failed.

The Big Idea (The "Why" Before the "How")

The entire book rests on one core insight: sleep is a learned skill, not an innate ability. Babies need to be taught to fall asleep independently, just like they'll later need to be taught to walk.

Giordano uses a memorable analogy: rocking a 12-week-old to sleep every single time is like carrying a 2-year-old everywhere instead of letting them learn to walk. In both cases, the child has the ability — but the parents are constantly "fixing it" for them instead of guiding them to do it on their own.

This reframing is important because it removes the guilt. You're not abandoning your baby. You're teaching them something.

The Two Pillars of the Method

Everything in the book flows from two interconnected principles:

Pillar 1: Structured Daytime Feeding. Get enough calories into the baby during the day so they don't need to eat at night. This is the metabolic foundation.

Pillar 2: Teaching Self-Soothing. The baby learns to fall asleep on their own in their crib — without props like rocking, swings, pacifiers, or nursing to sleep.

These two pillars create a virtuous cycle: a well-fed baby can sleep longer stretches, and a well-rested baby feeds more efficiently during the day.

Chapter-by-Chapter Breakdown

Chapter 1: Why Sleep Matters — The Case for Training

Key Messages: Newborns sleep 16 to 18 hours per day, but in short 2 to 4 hour bursts, both day and night. As they grow, these bursts naturally consolidate into longer periods. Sleep is crucial for the baby's health, growth, and brain development — but it's equally critical for parental well-being. Sleep-deprived parents are more impatient, less focused, and more prone to relationship strain. Many parents fall into "survival mode" patterns (rocking to sleep, co-sleeping out of exhaustion, feeding at every whimper) that work short-term but create dependencies that are harder to break later. Giordano compares the pre-training period to the days before starting a diet: every time you give in to bad habits, you make it harder to establish good ones later.

Note: Key insight. The book frames sleep training not as something you do to your baby, but something you do for your whole family. The goal is a well-rested baby AND well-rested parents, which means better parenting overall.

Chapter 2: The Basics of the Sleep Training Plan

This is the operational core of the book. The method is called "12 Hours by 12 Weeks" and it's built on simplicity and consistency.

Prerequisites — All Three Must Be Met: The minimum weight required is 9 pounds. The daily intake must be at least 24 ounces of milk or formula per day. The minimum age is 4 weeks for singletons, 8 weeks for twins, or 12 weeks for triplets, using adjusted age.

Why these matter: These aren't arbitrary. A baby under 9 lbs may not have the caloric reserves to go long stretches without eating. The age minimums exist because premature babies (common with multiples) need more time for digestive and neurological systems to mature. Always confirm with your pediatrician before starting.

Giordano recommends beginning the process as early as possible — ideally around 2 weeks old for getting into the feeding rhythm, even though formal sleep training doesn't start until the prerequisites are met.

Chapter 3: Step-by-Step — The Feeding Schedule

Step 1: Establish the 4-Hour Daytime Feeding Schedule

This is the foundation. Without this, nothing else works.

How it works: Divide the day into two 12-hour blocks (for example, 7 AM to 7 PM is day, 7 PM to 7 AM is night). Schedule 4 feedings during the daytime block, spaced 4 hours apart. An example schedule would be 7:00 AM, then 11:00 AM, then 3:00 PM, then 7:00 PM. You can flex by about 15 minutes in either direction, using the previous feed as the anchor.

Why 4 hours specifically? This is one of the more clever insights in the book. When babies eat small, frequent meals (every 1.5 to 2 hours), they never get truly hungry, so they never take a full feeding. By stretching to 4 hours, the baby arrives at each feeding genuinely hungry and eats a larger volume. This front-loads their caloric intake into the daytime — which is the key to dropping night feeds.

The Transition (This Won't Happen Overnight): If your baby currently eats every 2 to 3 hours, you gradually stretch the intervals. Each day, try to push feedings a little closer to the 4-hour target. During Step 1, focus exclusively on daytime feeds. Let the baby wake naturally at night and feed them whenever they wake up. Use the "daytime toolbox" (see below) to bridge the gap when baby fusses before the next feeding.

Warning: Important note for breastfeeding moms. Giordano recommends using expressed breast milk in bottles during training so you can measure exact ounces. Going 12 hours overnight without nursing or pumping can reduce milk supply. Many lactation consultants recommend keeping at least one night feed or pumping session until 6 to 10 months old. You can still achieve great sleep results with one remaining night feed.

Step 2: Gradually Eliminate Night Feedings

Once the daytime feeding schedule is solid, you start reducing night feeds. This is done gradually, not cold turkey.

The Method: Start with the second night feeding (it's usually the one where the baby drinks the least). Reduce the amount by half an ounce every three nights. If the baby naturally drinks less than the target amount, go with the lower amount and hold it for three nights. Once the second night feeding is eliminated, repeat the process for the first night feeding. The gradual reduction trains the baby's body to shift those calories to daytime.

Example Timeline: If the night feeding is currently 3 oz, reduce to 2.5 oz for 3 nights, then 2 oz for 3 nights, then 1.5 oz for 3 nights, then eliminate. If baby only drinks 1.5 oz when you offer 2.5, drop to 1.5 immediately and hold for 3 nights. Total timeline is roughly 2 to 3 weeks per feeding.

Why this works: It's basically metabolic retraining. The baby's body learns to expect (and store) more calories during the day and stops signaling hunger at night.

Step 3: Teach Self-Soothing — "The Limited Crying Solution"

This is Giordano's signature technique. She deliberately positions it as a middle ground between "no crying ever" (unrealistic for most families) and traditional "cry it out" (emotionally brutal for many parents).

The Process: Do your pre-sleep routine (bath, diaper, feeding, etc.). Put the baby in the crib awake (this is critical — not drowsy, not asleep, awake). Say goodnight and leave the room, closing the door. If baby doesn't cry — you're done. Celebrate. If baby cries: wait 3 to 5 minutes before going in. If the baby stops crying during that interval, restart the timer (the pause in crying shows self-soothing is happening). When you go in: do not pick the baby up. Soothe verbally, pat gently, but baby stays in the crib. Leave when the baby calms. If crying resumes, repeat the 3 to 5 minute wait. If crying escalates significantly, you can comfort the baby — but keep them in the crib.

Note: The mantra. "I cannot fix it for you, but I will go through it with you by your side."

Key Principles: The brief waiting period teaches the baby that they CAN self-soothe — you're giving them the opportunity to discover this skill. Extended crying is usually counterproductive; it exhausts both baby and parent without teaching anything. Consistency is everything. If you sometimes pick them up after 5 minutes and sometimes don't, you've just taught them that crying harder or longer eventually works. Most babies learn within a few days to a week.

Chapter 4: The Daytime — Naps and the "Toolbox"

Nap Structure

Giordano recommends two naps per day once the schedule is established. The morning nap is about 1 hour, typically mid-morning, between first and second feeding. The afternoon nap is about 2 hours, typically between second and third feeding. The total is about 3 hours of daytime sleep.

Note: Note from other experts. Many sleep consultants consider 2 naps insufficient for a 12-week-old. Most babies this age need 3 to 4 naps. If your baby seems overtired on 2 naps, trust your instincts and add a third short catnap before the evening routine.

The "Daytime Toolbox"

This is one of the most practically useful parts of the book. The toolbox is a collection of strategies to bridge the gap when baby fusses between scheduled feedings.

Toolbox items include the pacifier, which is useful for non-nutritive sucking needs, but use with caution against over-reliance. The bouncy seat or vibrating chair is for soothing during fussy periods, not for regular sleep. The swing is reserved for truly inconsolable moments (for example, colic or gas pain), not routine use. A diaper change can help because sometimes the distraction of a change is enough to reset a fussy baby. A change of scenery, such as moving to a different room or going outside, can also help. Gripe water is useful for gas and digestive discomfort. Tummy time serves double duty as developmental activity and distraction. Walking and bouncing provide physical movement to soothe.

Critical rule: These are tools, not habits. Use them situationally when the baby is truly distressed, not as default soothing mechanisms.

Chapter 5: The Sleep Environment

Creating the Ideal Setup: The baby should sleep in their own crib for all sleep in a consistent sleep location. Use blackout curtains to make the room dark, because darkness signals sleep. White noise can help, but shouldn't be so loud it becomes a prop. The temperature should be a comfortable room temperature of 68 to 72 degrees Fahrenheit (20 to 22 degrees Celsius). During the day, keep the house bright and at normal noise levels. During evening, dim lights and reduce stimulation to maintain a clear day versus night distinction.

Bedtime Routine (keep it short and consistent): The routine should be brief, predictable, and calming. Examples include a warm bath, then diaper change, then pajamas, then a short feeding, then place in crib awake. The same sequence every single night provides a clear signal that sleep is coming. Both parents should be on the same page and follow the same routine.

Sleepy Cues to Watch For: Yawning, rubbing eyes, becoming less active or losing interest in toys, fussiness that escalates, and staring into space.

When you see these cues, it's time to start the bedtime or naptime routine. Missing the window leads to overtiredness, which paradoxically makes it harder to fall asleep.

Chapter 6: Maintaining the Schedule — The Jell-O Analogy

Giordano uses a great metaphor here: think of the new routine as Jell-O setting in the fridge. It needs time to solidify before you can mess with it.

The First 6 Months: Be Rigid. Maintain the schedule as consistently as possible. When disruptions happen (doctor's appointment, travel), try to stay as close to the feeding and sleep cycles as you can. After a disruption, get back on schedule as quickly as possible.

After 6 Months: Introduce Flexibility. Once the routine is firmly established, small deviations won't derail everything. Sleeping in on a weekend, staying out a bit later for an event — these become manageable. Keep deviations to one day max to prevent the routine from unraveling.

Travel and Vacations: Bring familiar items such as a portable crib, favorite sleep toy or blanket, and white noise machine. Maintain bedtime rituals even in unfamiliar environments. Expect some regression — the baby may wake up in a new environment. Upon returning home, get back on schedule immediately.

Chapter 7: Special Situations — Colic, Reflux, and Special Needs

Reflux: Position babies at an incline using bouncy seats. The core method remains the same — structured feeding, gradual night weaning, self-soothing. As reflux improves (usually by 4 to 6 months), transition to flat sleeping.

Colic: The toolbox becomes even more important during colicky periods. Maintain the structure as much as possible, but accept that colic periods will be harder. Colic typically peaks around 6 weeks and resolves by 3 to 4 months.

Twins and Multiples: The method was literally designed for multiples — this is its origin story. Train both babies simultaneously on the same schedule. Same feeding times, same bedtime, same routine.

Babies with Special Needs: The method may take longer and require more patience. Work closely with your pediatrician to adapt the specifics.

Chapter 8: Common Challenges and Sleep Regressions

Sleep Regressions: These are periods when a baby who was sleeping well suddenly starts waking up again. They typically coincide with developmental milestones (rolling over, sitting up, teething). The number one rule: stay consistent. Don't abandon the plan because of a regression. Regressions are temporary.

Growth Spurts: Baby may seem hungrier during the day — increase daytime feeding volume, not frequency. Don't reintroduce night feeds that have already been eliminated. Growth spurts typically last 2 to 3 days.

Teething: Use appropriate pain relief as recommended by your pediatrician. Maintain the routine — comfort the baby but don't create new sleep associations.

Illness: When a baby is sick, comfort takes priority over schedule. Once recovered, get back on schedule quickly.

The Complete Method at a Glance

Sample Daily Schedule (using 7 AM to 7 PM window):

At 7:00 AM, the baby wakes up and has their first feeding. From 8:00 to 9:00 AM is awake and play time. From 9:00 to 10:00 AM is the morning nap, about 1 hour. From 10:00 to 11:00 AM is awake and play time. At 11:00 AM is the second feeding. From 12:00 to 2:00 PM is the afternoon nap, about 2 hours. From 2:00 to 3:00 PM is awake and play time. At 3:00 PM is the third feeding. From 3:30 to 5:30 PM is awake and play time. From 5:30 to 6:00 PM is an optional short catnap. From 6:00 to 6:45 PM is the bedtime routine (bath, pajamas, etc.). At 7:00 PM is the fourth feeding, and the baby is placed in the crib awake. From 7:00 PM to 7:00 AM is sleep, 12 hours.

Note: This is the target schedule. You work toward this gradually, not all at once.

Key Action Items & Checklists

Pre-Training Readiness Checklist

Baby weighs at least 9 lbs. Baby consumes at least 24 oz per day. Baby meets minimum age: 4 weeks for singletons, 8 weeks for twins, or 12 weeks for triplets. Pediatrician has given the green light. Both parents have read the method and are on the same page. You're mentally prepared to commit for at least 2 to 3 weeks. Nursery or sleep space is set up with crib, blackout curtains, and appropriate temperature.

Equipment & Supplies Checklist

You will need a crib in a dedicated sleep space, blackout curtains, a white noise machine (optional but helpful), bottles (even if breastfeeding — for measured expressed milk during training), a breast pump (if breastfeeding), a bouncy seat or vibrating chair (for the toolbox), a swing (for emergency soothing, not routine use), pacifiers (use judiciously), gripe water (for gas), a portable crib or travel crib (for maintaining routine away from home), and a sleep log or tracking chart.

Step 1 Checklist: Daytime Feeding Schedule

Choose your 12-hour daytime window (for example, 7 AM to 7 PM). Map out 4 feeding times, 4 hours apart. Start gradually stretching from current feeding interval toward 4 hours. Use toolbox items to bridge gaps when baby fusses before next feeding. Track ounces consumed at each feeding. Confirm baby is getting at least 24 oz during the 12-hour day window. Let baby wake naturally at night and feed on demand (for now).

Step 2 Checklist: Eliminating Night Feedings

Identify which night feeding the baby takes the least at (start there). Reduce that feeding by 0.5 oz. Hold the new amount for 3 consecutive nights. Continue reducing by 0.5 oz every 3 nights until eliminated. If baby voluntarily drinks less than target, follow their lead. Once the first night feed is gone, repeat for the remaining night feed. Monitor that daytime intake stays at or above 24 oz to compensate.

Step 3 Checklist: Teaching Self-Soothing

Establish a short, consistent bedtime routine (5 to 15 minutes). Place baby in crib awake (not drowsy, not asleep). Say goodnight and leave the room. If crying: wait 3 to 5 minutes before checking. If crying stops during the wait: restart the timer. When checking: do NOT pick up the baby. Pat, shush, soothe verbally. Leave when baby calms. Repeat if crying resumes. Apply the same method for naps. Stay consistent — both parents, every sleep time, every day.

Honest Cautions & Balanced Perspective

Warning: Things to be aware of. Breastfeeding concerns: Going 12 hours without nursing or pumping can significantly reduce milk supply. Many lactation consultants suggest keeping one night feed or pumping session until 6 to 10 months. Nap recommendations may be insufficient: Two naps totaling 3 hours may not be enough for a 12-week-old. Most sleep experts recommend 3 to 4 naps at that age. Not all babies respond the same to "limited crying." Some babies do not calm without being picked up. If checks seem to escalate rather than soothe, you may need to adapt. The 12-week timeline is ambitious. It works for many families, but some babies may take longer. That's okay. Safe sleep guidelines: The book was originally published in 2006. Always follow current AAP safe sleep recommendations. Every baby is different. You may need to combine elements of this approach with other methods. That's not failure — it's parenting.

Key Takeaways — The TL;DR

Sleep is a skill you teach, not a trait babies have. Remove the guilt. Structured daytime feeding is the foundation: 4 feeds, 4 hours apart, 24 or more ounces during the day. Gradually wean night feeds by reducing 0.5 oz every 3 nights. Don't go cold turkey. Put the baby down awake. This is the single most important habit to build. The Limited Crying Solution means wait 3 to 5 minutes, soothe without picking up, leave when calm. Consistency is everything. Both parents, every time, no exceptions (especially the first 6 months). Use the toolbox to bridge gaps — pacifier, bouncy seat, change of scenery — but don't let tools become dependencies. Regressions are normal and temporary. Stay the course. The Jell-O analogy: Let the routine set before you start bending it. Trust your baby — they're more capable of self-soothing than you think. And trust yourself — you know your baby better than any book does.
